Indian History - Mughal Empire

The Mughal Empire was one of the greatest empires in Indian history. It was founded by Babur in 1526 after his victory in the First Battle of Panipat against Ibrahim Lodi.

Key Rulers:

1. Babur (1526-1530)
- Founder of the Mughal Empire
- Defeated Ibrahim Lodi at the First Battle of Panipat in 1526
- Wrote his autobiography "Baburnama" in Chagatai Turkish
- Introduced gunpowder warfare to India

2. Humayun (1530-1540, 1555-1556)
- Son of Babur
- Lost his empire to Sher Shah Suri in 1540
- Regained the throne in 1555 with Persian help
- Died in 1556 after falling from the stairs of his library

3. Akbar (1556-1605)
- Greatest Mughal emperor
- Expanded the empire significantly
- Introduced the Mansabdari system for military and civil administration
- Promoted religious tolerance through Din-i-Ilahi
- Built Fatehpur Sikri as his capital
- Abolished the Jizya tax on non-Muslims

4. Jahangir (1605-1627)
- Son of Akbar
- Married Nur Jahan who wielded significant political power
- Known for his love of art and nature
- Issued the "Chain of Justice" for common people to seek justice

5. Shah Jahan (1628-1658)
- Built the Taj Mahal in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal
- Known as the "Engineer King" for his architectural achievements
- Built the Red Fort and Jama Masjid in Delhi
- His reign is considered the golden age of Mughal architecture

6. Aurangzeb (1658-1707)
- Last great Mughal emperor
- Expanded the empire to its greatest territorial extent
- Reimposed the Jizya tax
- His religious policies led to conflicts with Rajputs, Marathas, and Sikhs
- After his death, the Mughal Empire began to decline

Administrative System:

The Mughal administrative system was highly centralized. The emperor was the supreme authority. The empire was divided into Subahs (provinces), which were further divided into Sarkars and Parganas.

The Mansabdari system introduced by Akbar was a unique feature. Mansabdars were military commanders and civil administrators who were ranked based on their Zat (personal rank) and Sawar (cavalry rank).

Revenue System:

Akbar's revenue minister Todar Mal introduced the Zabti system, also known as the Bandobast system. Land was measured and classified based on productivity. Revenue was fixed as one-third of the average produce.

Cultural Contributions:

The Mughals made significant contributions to Indian art, architecture, literature, and music. They introduced Persian culture and language to India. Miniature painting flourished under their patronage. The Mughal gardens with their char-bagh design became iconic.

Important Facts for Competitive Exams:

- The Mughal Empire lasted from 1526 to 1857
- The last Mughal emperor was Bahadur Shah Zafar II
- The empire reached its peak under Aurangzeb covering most of the Indian subcontinent
- Mughal architecture is a blend of Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles
- The official language of the Mughal court was Persian
- The Mughals introduced new crops like tobacco and maize to India
